Our Selection Criteria (WIP)
Exploring a structured selection rubric that is internal only — where scoring and weights are not shared in detail externally. For public-facing messaging, we’ll use behavioral descriptors that convey what Apple values without disclosing the scoring mechanics. (This is still under review).

Key decision from the team: externally, we share what we looked for. We never share how we scored it. The rubric, weights, and individual scores stay with selection team.


Messaging Principles for Apple
Lead with community impact, not Apple's objectives. Frame around what recognized developers do for the broader ecosystem (mentoring, educating, building, organizing, not what they do for Apple.
Let developers tell their own story. Developer-authored blog posts timed to announcement. Aligned to PR guidance. Auth0 and Docker both use this model (members share in their own words how recognition benefits their community). This should feel organic, not Apple-scripted.
Be transparent about what we value, not how we score. The behavioral descriptors above are shareable. The rubric, weights, and numerical scores are not. This mirrors industry practice, Microsoft and Google publish qualitative criteria but never scoring mechanics.
Acknowledge the spectrum of community leadership. Following the emerging markets discussion: community impact doesn't require a large global following. Messaging should convey that Apple recognizes impact at all scales, a deeply engaged regional community is as valuable as a large global audience.
Acknowledge that community leadership looks different everywhere. Following PR input and the team's emerging markets work: a developer in Southeast Asia with a small but deeply engaged community is as impactful as someone with a massive global following. Messaging should convey that Apple recognizes impact at all scales.
Frame aspiration, not exclusion. The most successful programs (AWS, Salesforce) communicate "here's how you can get involved" rather than "here's who made the cut." Messaging should make non-selected developers feel inspired, not shut out.
